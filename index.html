Professional Self-Assessment

When I began the Computer Science program, I was interested in technology in a general sense. Over time, that interest became more focused. Through coursework and the process of building this ePortfolio, I realized that I am most interested in infrastructure and enterprise-level systems, especially in structured environments such as state or public sector organizations. I am drawn to systems that need to be reliable, secure, and carefully designed rather than built quickly and revised later.

Throughout the program, I developed the ability to think about computing systems beyond just whether they function. In projects involving databases, service layers, and full-stack development, I learned to evaluate structure, maintainability, and long-term impact. In CS 499, enhancing previous artifacts required me to step back and analyze my own design decisions. I identified areas where modularity could be improved, where efficiency could be refined, and where documentation could better support collaboration. This process reflects the program outcome of designing and evaluating computing solutions while managing trade-offs in structure and performance.

Communication has also been an important area of growth. In discussion assignments and the informal code review video, I practiced explaining technical concepts in a way that made sense to different audiences. Whether discussing neural networks, reinforcement learning, or bias in AI systems, I focused on clarity and context rather than assuming technical knowledge. In professional environments, especially in enterprise or public systems, the ability to communicate clearly with managers, stakeholders, and team members is essential. The code review process in particular helped me strengthen my ability to justify design decisions and explain planned enhancements logically and professionally.

My understanding of algorithms and data structures deepened as I examined efficiency, state-based models, and optimization strategies. In reinforcement learning discussions, I analyzed how systems make decisions based on states and rewards. In later modules, I evaluated how AI systems like AlphaGo Zero improved performance through structured iteration and self-play. These experiences reinforced the importance of efficiency, scalability, and careful evaluation of algorithmic design choices. Rather than seeing algorithms as abstract theory, I began viewing them as practical tools that shape how systems behave under real constraints.

Security and accountability have become central to how I think about computing. In discussions about AI bias, privacy regulations, and responsible research, I consistently questioned how systems affect users. I learned that building software is not only about solving technical problems but also about anticipating unintended consequences. This mindset carries into database design, input validation, and system architecture. Protecting data, minimizing unnecessary exposure, and planning for future risks are especially important in enterprise and public-sector systems, where trust and compliance matter.

The artifacts in this portfolio reflect that progression. The software design and engineering enhancement demonstrates improved modularity and structure. The algorithms and data structures enhancement focuses on computational reasoning and efficiency. The database enhancement highlights structured data management and system integration. Together, these artifacts show growth not only in technical implementation but also in design evaluation, communication, and security awareness.

Completing the Computer Science program has shaped both my technical skill set and my professional goals. I am interested in contributing to infrastructure and enterprise systems that require stability, careful planning, and long-term accountability. I value environments where systems must work reliably, where decisions are documented, and where security and privacy are treated as foundational rather than optional.

This ePortfolio represents how I approach computing problems: analytically, thoughtfully, and with attention to long-term impact. It reflects both what I have built and how I have learned to think as a computer science professional.

This portfolio demonstrates my achievement of the program learning outcomes. Through code reviews and structured enhancements, I practiced building collaborative environments and supporting stakeholder decision making. By presenting artifacts, narratives, and visual demonstrations, I strengthened my professional written and oral communication skills. My enhancements required designing and evaluating computing solutions using algorithmic principles while managing design trade-offs in performance and structure. I applied industry-relevant tools and techniques, including full-stack development, database integration, and modular architecture. Finally, I developed a stronger security mindset by emphasizing data validation, privacy considerations, and responsible system design throughout my projects.

Artifact 1: Software Design and Engineering

This artifact is based on a project originally created in CS 340: Advanced Programming Concepts. The application was designed to manage and query animal shelter data using a MongoDB backend and a Python-based service layer. The original version focused on core CRUD functionality and correct interaction with the database.

I selected this artifact for my ePortfolio because it demonstrates my ability to design and structure a complete software solution using modular components. The enhancement for this category focused on improving the overall design, readability, and maintainability of the application. These changes included reorganizing functions for clearer separation of responsibilities, improving naming conventions, and adding structured documentation to better support future development and collaboration.

Through this enhancement, I demonstrated software engineering skills such as modular design, abstraction, and code organization. I also applied principles of the software development lifecycle by evaluating the original design, identifying areas for improvement, and implementing changes that improve long-term usability without altering core functionality.
